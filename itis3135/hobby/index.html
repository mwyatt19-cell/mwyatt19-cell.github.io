About Me

Field hockey is a globally beloved team sport played on grass, turf, or indoor surfaces, emphasizing agility, strategy, and teamwork. From my first time holding a stick, I was captivated by how the sport requires both quick thinking and physical coordination. Whether it’s sprinting down the field or weaving the ball between defenders, every moment feels exciting.

I began playing field hockey in middle school and quickly grew to appreciate the strong sense of community within the sport. Practices with teammates and weekend games created friendships that extended beyond the field. USA Field Hockey promotes values like respect, inclusion, and sportsmanship, which have shaped how I approach competition and teamwork in my everyday life.

Beyond the athletic challenge, field hockey has taught me discipline, resilience, and communication. Training regularly helped me balance school, practices, and personal goals, and being part of a team reinforced how important encouragement and collaboration are. Field hockey isn’t just a hobby — it’s a part of who I am.

What/Home Page

Field hockey is a fast-paced sport played by two teams of eleven players, each using a curved stick to hit a small, hard ball into the opponent’s goal. The game is played internationally and is part of the Olympic Games. In the United States, clubs, schools, and youth leagues support players at every level, from beginners to highly competitive athletes.

USA Field Hockey is the national governing body for the sport in the U.S., providing resources, coaching certification, and programs to grow participation. Through membership clubs, tournaments, and community outreach, the organization helps players of all ages learn the rules, refine skills, and deepen their appreciation for the game. Field hockey’s blend of speed, finesse, and strategy makes it exciting to play and exciting to watch.

You don’t need to be an expert to enjoy field hockey. Whether practicing dribbling in your backyard, joining a local rec league, or cheering on teams at tournaments, there are many ways to engage with the sport. At its core, field hockey is about passion, connection, and the joy of play.

Seasons

In the United States, field hockey is commonly a fall sport for high school and collegiate teams, with practices beginning in late summer and competitive matches running through autumn. During this time, athletes train regularly to prepare for league play and regional tournaments, often balancing schoolwork with demanding schedules.

Outside of the main season, many players continue working on their skills through spring leagues, summer camps, and club programs. Many youth clubs host clinics and off-season practices that focus on fitness, stick skills, and game understanding. Year-round involvement helps players stay sharp and ready for the competitive season.

Organized events like camps and tournaments are often scheduled during school breaks to allow players from different regions to meet and compete. By participating in multiple seasonal opportunities, players can develop both technically and socially, expanding their network within the field hockey community.

Where to Play

Field hockey is played across the United States in a variety of settings, from school turf fields to dedicated club facilities. Many high schools and colleges have modern turf fields designed to enhance speed of play and reduce injuries. These locations often serve as social hubs where teammates gather and build community.

USA Field Hockey supports more than 300 member clubs nationwide, offering places where players of all ages can learn and compete. Club locations are often found in regions with larger field hockey communities, such as the Northeast, Mid-Atlantic, and Midwest, but opportunities exist in many other areas as well.

Even if your area doesn’t have a formal field hockey club, many parks and athletic complexes have open fields where players can practice stick skills or organize informal matches. Indoor facilities also provide winter training options in regions that experience cold weather, ensuring year‑round engagement with the sport.

How to Play

Getting started with field hockey is simple: you need a field hockey stick, a ball, protective equipment like shin guards and a mouthguard, and a willingness to learn. USA Field Hockey offers beginner guides that explain how to hold the stick correctly and practice basic skills like dribbling, passing, and shooting.

Local clubs and youth programs often host introductory sessions where new players can learn in a supportive environment. These clinics typically focus on fundamentals, including footwork, ball control, and teamwork. Coaches emphasize proper technique and safety to build confidence and enjoyment.

Field hockey is a fast-paced team sport played on grass, turf, or indoor courts, where two teams of eleven players each use a curved stick to hit a small, hard ball with the goal of scoring in the opposing team’s net. According to USA Field Hockey, players must use only the flat side of the stick to strike the ball, and the game is typically divided into four quarters of 15 minutes each at the international level. Positions include forwards, midfielders, defenders, and a goalkeeper, each with specialized roles in both offense and defense.
